## About spring types
Japanese hot springs are classified by their dissolved minerals, and each type is traditionally associated with different qualities and benefits. The notes below describe what each type is commonly said to be good for, drawing on the published guidance of the Japan Secret Hot Springs Association. These are traditional and general associations, not medical guarantees; anyone with a health condition should consult a doctor. The spring-type names below match the labels used to describe each onsen.

**Source:** Japan Secret Hot Springs Association — https://www.hitou.or.jp/static/spa_guide_type , https://www.hitou.or.jp/static/spa_guide_efficacy

## Simple Spring
A mild spring with a relatively low mineral content (less than 1,000 mg of dissolved minerals per kg of water). Because it is low in irritation, it is gentle and easy on the skin and is often recommended for sensitive skin, older bathers, and first-time onsen visitors. It is widely used and traditionally associated with relaxation and easing general fatigue, muscle stiffness, and nerve pain.

## Bicarbonate Spring
Often called a "beauty bath" (bijin-no-yu) because it can leave skin feeling smooth and soft. The bicarbonate helps soften the skin and remove surface impurities. It is traditionally associated with smoother skin and a refreshed feeling after bathing, and is popular with those seeking a cosmetic, skin-softening soak.

## Chloride Spring
A salt-rich spring with a composition similar to seawater. The salt coats the skin, helps suppress sweating, and helps the body retain heat, so the warming effect tends to linger well after you leave the bath — which is why it is nicknamed the "heat bath." It is traditionally associated with keeping the body warm, easing chills, and soothing minor cuts.

## Sulfate Spring
A spring containing sulfate minerals, with a faintly bitter taste. Traditionally called the "wound bath," it is associated with soothing the skin and helping minor cuts, and with supporting circulation. It comes in several subtypes depending on the dominant mineral, and is often grouped with chloride and bicarbonate springs as a well-rounded, gentle mineral bath.

## Iron Spring
Contains iron, which oxidises on contact with air and can turn the water a reddish or brownish colour. It has a strong warming effect and is traditionally associated with warming the body; it is also associated with anemia and menstrual disorders. The distinctive coloured water comes from the minerals, not impurities.

## Sulfur Spring
Known for its distinctive "rotten egg" smell and sometimes milky, cloudy water. Sulfur softens hardened skin and is traditionally associated with skin conditions such as acne and blemishes, as well as chronic constipation, high blood pressure, arteriosclerosis, and gout. Because sulfur springs can release hydrogen sulfide gas, well-ventilated bathing is important. The water can be strong and slightly drying, so rinsing and moisturising afterward can help sensitive skin.

## Acidic Spring
A strongly acidic spring with powerful antibacterial qualities. It is traditionally associated with chronic skin conditions, but its strength is harsh on sensitive skin and eyes and is not suitable for everyone — rinse with fresh water after bathing. Often found in volcanic areas and prized for its potency.

## Radon Spring
Also called a "radium bath," it contains only trace amounts of naturally occurring radioactivity. It is traditionally associated with joint pain, muscle pain, and nerve pain, as well as skin conditions and high blood pressure, and is usually a gentle, mild-feeling, restorative soak.

## Iodine Spring
A spring containing iodine, which can give the water a yellowish tint. It is a less common type with its own distinctive character, valued as a mineral-rich soak.

## Carbon Dioxide Spring
Free carbon dioxide forms fine bubbles that cling to the skin, giving a tingling, fizzy feel — earning it the nicknames "bubble bath" and "ramune bath." The carbon dioxide dilates the blood vessels and is traditionally associated with supporting circulation and lowering blood pressure, and it provides a gentle warming effect even at lower water temperatures, making it comfortable for longer soaks.

## Aluminium Spring
A spring containing aluminium minerals. It tends to be colourless to slightly yellow and acidic, with strong antibacterial (bactericidal) qualities and an astringent, tightening feel on the skin. A less common type with a distinctive character.

## Copper-Iron Spring
A mineral spring containing both copper and iron. It oxidises to leave yellow-to-brown sediment and generates a warming effect, though the minerals can reduce how well soap lathers. Like iron springs, the water can take on colour from the dissolved minerals; it is a rarer, mineral-rich bath.

## Other
Covers springs that do not fall neatly into the standard mineral categories, or whose classification is not specified. The character of these springs varies, so it is best to enjoy each on its own terms.

## General benefits and important cautions
Beyond the specific spring types, hot-spring bathing in general is traditionally associated with relief from nerve pain, muscle pain, joint pain, stiff shoulders, bruises and sprains, poor circulation, chronic digestive complaints, fatigue, and recovery after illness. The benefits come from the dissolved minerals, the warmth and water pressure, and the simple act of getting away from daily life.

Some people should not bathe in hot springs. Bathing is generally discouraged during acute illness with a fever, active tuberculosis, serious heart or kidney disease, respiratory failure, bleeding conditions, severe anemia, malignant disease, and during pregnancy (especially the early and late stages). Certain spring types carry their own cautions — for example, strongly acidic and sulfur springs are not suitable for sensitive skin. This is general information, not medical advice; if you have a health condition, check with your doctor before bathing.
