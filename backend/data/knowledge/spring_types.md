## About spring types
Japanese hot springs are classified by their dissolved minerals, and each type is traditionally associated with different qualities and benefits. The notes below describe what each type is commonly said to be good for. These are traditional associations rather than medical guarantees, and anyone with a health condition should consult a doctor. The spring-type names below match the labels used to describe each onsen.

## Simple Spring
A mild spring with relatively low mineral content. Gentle and easy on the skin, it is often recommended for sensitive skin, older bathers, and first-time onsen visitors. It is traditionally associated with relaxation and easing general fatigue, muscle stiffness, and nerve pain. Because it is mild, it tends to feel soft and unintimidating.

## Bicarbonate Spring
Often called a "beauty bath" (bijin-no-yu) because it can leave skin feeling smooth and soft. The bicarbonate helps emulsify oils and gently cleanses the skin's surface. It is traditionally associated with smoother skin and a refreshed feeling after bathing, and is popular with those seeking a cosmetic, skin-softening soak.

## Chloride Spring
A salt-rich spring. The salt coats the skin and helps the body retain heat, so the warming effect tends to linger well after you leave the bath. It is traditionally associated with keeping the body warm, easing chills, and soothing minor cuts. A good choice when you want lasting warmth.

## Sulfate Spring
A spring containing sulfate minerals. It is traditionally associated with supporting circulation and soothing the skin, and is sometimes linked with helping minor cuts and dry skin. Often grouped with chloride and bicarbonate springs as a well-rounded, gentle mineral bath.

## Iron Spring
Contains iron, which oxidises on contact with air and can turn the water a reddish or brownish colour. It is traditionally associated with warming the body. The distinctive coloured water is part of its character; the colour comes from the minerals, not impurities.

## Sulfur Spring
Known for its distinctive "rotten egg" smell and sometimes milky, cloudy water. Sulfur springs are traditionally associated with skin conditions and are among the most characterful onsen. The water can be strong and slightly drying, so rinsing off afterward and moisturising can help sensitive skin.

## Acidic Spring
A strongly acidic spring with antibacterial qualities. It is traditionally associated with skin conditions, but the strength can be harsh on sensitive skin or eyes, so it is wise to rinse with fresh water after bathing. Often found in volcanic areas and prized for its potency.

## Radon Spring
Contains trace amounts of naturally occurring radon. It is traditionally associated with general wellness and relaxation, and is usually a gentle, mild-feeling bath. The radon content is very low and the appeal is the calm, restorative soak.

## Iodine Spring
A spring containing iodine, which can give the water a yellowish tint. It is a less common type with its own distinctive character, traditionally valued as a mineral-rich soak.

## Carbon Dioxide Spring
Carbon dioxide forms fine bubbles that cling to the skin, giving a tingling, fizzy feel. It is traditionally associated with supporting circulation and provides a gentle warming effect even at lower water temperatures, making it comfortable for longer soaks.

## Aluminium Spring
A spring containing aluminium minerals, which give it an astringent quality. It is a less common type with a characteristically tightening feel on the skin.

## Copper-Iron Spring
A mineral spring containing both copper and iron. It is a rarer type, valued as a distinctive, mineral-rich bath, and like iron springs the water can take on colour from the dissolved minerals.

## Other
Covers springs that do not fall neatly into the standard mineral categories, or whose classification is not specified. The character of these springs varies, so it is best to enjoy each on its own terms.
