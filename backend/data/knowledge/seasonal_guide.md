## Onsen through the seasons
Onsen are enjoyed year-round, and each season offers something different — different scenery, seasonal foods, and even special seasonal baths. The contrast between the hot water and the outside air is a big part of the appeal. Many travellers match the season to the experience they want, soaking amid cherry blossoms, autumn leaves, or falling snow.

**Source:** U-U.co.jp — https://u-u.co.jp/blog/japan_hot_spring_guide/postid_8224/

## Spring
In spring, cherry blossoms and fresh greenery make beautiful backdrops for a bath, and flower-viewing baths (hanami-yu) are popular at onsen such as Kusatsu and Gero. The mild warmth suits a relaxed, restorative soak, and some places offer seasonal mugwort baths (yomogi-yu). Spring bathing is also said to help refresh the body and ease hay-fever symptoms. Travellers often head to areas like Izu, Atami, and Hakone to enjoy blossoms with their bath.

**Source:** U-U.co.jp — https://u-u.co.jp/blog/japan_hot_spring_guide/postid_8224/ ; Milltalk (community) — https://milltalk.jp/boards/120465

## Summer
Summer onsen visits are best in cooler high-altitude and mountain-stream settings, where the air stays fresh — areas such as Kurokawa and Hakone, and elevated regions like Nagano and Hokkaido, make good retreats. Lowland baths in summer call for heat-stroke care, so stay well hydrated before and after bathing. A soak can also relieve summer fatigue and the chill that comes from too much air conditioning.

**Source:** U-U.co.jp — https://u-u.co.jp/blog/japan_hot_spring_guide/postid_8224/ ; Milltalk (community) — https://milltalk.jp/boards/120465

## Autumn
Autumn is often called the best season for onsen, with high visitor satisfaction. Mountain areas and towns such as Kinosaki turn red and gold with maple leaves (koyo), and an outdoor bath framed by autumn foliage is a highlight. The season's cuisine — mushrooms, chestnuts, and the autumn harvest — features in onsen meals, and some places offer mandarin-orange baths (mikan-yu). Because it is popular, booking early is wise. Nikko, Kusatsu, and Kurokawa are among the areas travellers favour for fall colour.

**Source:** U-U.co.jp — https://u-u.co.jp/blog/japan_hot_spring_guide/postid_8224/ ; Milltalk (community) — https://milltalk.jp/boards/120465

## Winter
Winter is the classic onsen season. Soaking in an outdoor bath (rotenburo) while snow falls — a snow-viewing bath, or yukimi-buro — is a signature experience, and the heat penetrates deeply into a body stiff with cold. Towns such as Arima, Dogo, and Kusatsu are acclaimed for winter bathing, while Yufuin and the northeastern (Tohoku) regions are popular for snowy scenery. Citron baths (yuzu-yu) are a warming winter tradition. Take care with the sharp temperature change between the cold air and hot water, which can stress the heart — move gradually and warm up slowly.

**Source:** U-U.co.jp — https://u-u.co.jp/blog/japan_hot_spring_guide/postid_8224/ ; Milltalk (community) — https://milltalk.jp/boards/120465

## When to go — crowds and cost
If you want to avoid crowds, the quieter periods tend to be mid-January through February, after Golden Week in May, early September, and early December. Booking two to three months ahead generally gives the best rates, though occasional last-minute deals appear. As always, gentle, moderate bathing suits children and older visitors best.
