## Step 1 — Undress in the changing room
Enter the correct changing room — red noren curtains usually mark the women's bath and blue the men's, with 女 for women and 男 for men. Remove all your clothing and place it in a basket or locker. Take only your small towel into the bathing area.

## Step 2 — Rinse before entering (kakeyu)
Before getting into the bath, rinse your body. Sit at a washing station and use the shower, or scoop water from the bath with a bucket and pour it over yourself, starting from your feet and working up. This both cleans you and lets your body adjust to the hot water. Pouring some water over your head and neck as well helps prevent the sudden rise in blood pressure and the dizziness that can come from entering hot water — a recommended precaution.

**Source:** Japan Onsen Association — https://www.spa.or.jp/onsen/524/

## Step 3 — Wash your body
Sit on the stool at the washing station and wash thoroughly with soap and shampoo. Rinse off all suds completely so no soap reaches the communal bath. Keep the water and bubbles contained at your station out of consideration for others.

## Step 4 — Soak slowly
Enter the bath gently and quietly without splashing. Keep your small towel out of the water, resting it on your head or the side. The water is hot: around 41°C — a comfortable "warm bath" — is the most popular temperature, while hotter baths of 43°C and above feel invigorating but are more taxing on the body. Ease in and soak calmly. Do not put your head under or swim.

**Source:** Japan Onsen Association — https://www.spa.or.jp/onsen/524/

## Step 5 — Rest between soaks
If you feel hot or light-headed, step out and sit on the edge or cool off for a few minutes, then return. Alternating short soaks with rest is normal and comfortable. Listen to your body and do not overdo the heat.

## Step 6 — Finish and dry off
When you are done, you can pat off excess water with your small towel before returning to the changing room — this keeps the floor dry. Whether to rinse off the mineral water afterward is personal preference; some leave it on to enjoy the spring's effects. Dress, drink water, and rest. Many people enjoy a cold drink after bathing.

## A note on heat and your health
Hotter water places more strain on the body. Very hot baths (43°C and above) call for particular care if you have high blood pressure or a heart condition, and deeper tubs add water pressure, so people with heart conditions should be cautious about deep, full-body immersion. Gentler alternatives include a "warm bath" around 41°C, a longer soak in lukewarm water (about 36–38°C), or half-body and lower-body bathing, which are easier on the body while still warming you and supporting circulation. If you feel unwell, get out and rest.
