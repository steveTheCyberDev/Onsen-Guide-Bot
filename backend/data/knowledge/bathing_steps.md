## Step 1 — Undress in the changing room
Enter the correct changing room — red noren curtains usually mark the women's bath and blue the men's, with 女 for women and 男 for men. Remove all your clothing and place it in a basket or locker. Take only your small towel into the bathing area. It is also wise to drink a cup of water before bathing, as you lose fluid in the heat. Do not bathe straight after drinking alcohol, immediately before or after a meal, or right after strenuous exercise — rest first.

**Source:** Japan Onsen Association — https://www.spa.or.jp/onsen/1632/ ; Japan Secret Hot Springs Association — https://www.hitou.or.jp/static/spa_guide_howto_bathe

## Step 2 — Rinse before entering (kakeyu)
Before getting into the bath, rinse your body. Sit at a washing station and use the shower, or scoop water from the bath with a bucket and pour it over yourself, starting from your feet and working up. Pouring from the feet upward, away from the heart, lets your body acclimatise gradually and helps prevent dizziness. Pouring some water over your head and neck as well (a practice called kaburi-yu) gently dilates the blood vessels and further guards against the sudden blood-pressure change and light-headedness that can come from entering hot water.

**Source:** Japan Onsen Association — https://www.spa.or.jp/onsen/524/ ; Japan Secret Hot Springs Association — https://www.hitou.or.jp/static/spa_guide_howto_bathe

## Step 3 — Wash your body
Sit on the stool at the washing station and wash thoroughly with soap and shampoo. Stay seated while you wash your hair and body — standing up to shampoo can splash soapy water onto the people around you. Rinse off all suds completely so no soap reaches the communal bath, and return basins and stools to where you found them.

**Source:** Japan Secret Hot Springs Association — https://www.hitou.or.jp/static/spa_guide_howto_bathe

## Step 4 — Soak slowly
Enter the bath gently and quietly without splashing. Keep your small towel out of the water, resting it on your head or the side. The water is hot: around 41°C — a comfortable "warm bath" — is the most popular temperature, while hotter baths of 42–43°C and above feel invigorating but are more taxing on the body. Keep your movements gentle and quiet. Start with a short soak of about 3–10 minutes and build up to 15–20 minutes as you get used to it, rather than staying in a long time on your first soak. Do not put your head under or swim.

**Source:** Japan Onsen Association — https://www.spa.or.jp/onsen/524/ , https://www.spa.or.jp/onsen/1632/ ; Japan Secret Hot Springs Association — https://www.hitou.or.jp/static/spa_guide_howto_bathe

## Step 5 — Rest between soaks
If you feel hot or light-headed, step out and sit on the edge or cool off for a few minutes, then return. Alternating short soaks with rest is normal and comfortable. Listen to your body and do not overdo the heat.

## Step 6 — Finish and dry off
Get out slowly to avoid dizziness. Pat off excess water with your small towel before returning to the changing room — this keeps the floor dry. As a rule it is best not to rinse off the spring water, so your skin keeps the mineral benefits; the exception is sensitive skin, or strongly acidic or sulfur springs, where you should rinse with fresh water afterward. Then dress, rehydrate, and rest quietly for around 30 minutes, as your blood pressure settles and your body has used energy. Take care not to get chilled.

**Source:** Japan Onsen Association — https://www.spa.or.jp/onsen/1632/ ; Japan Secret Hot Springs Association — https://www.hitou.or.jp/static/spa_guide_howto_bathe

## A note on heat and your health
Hotter water places more strain on the body. Hot baths of around 42°C and above call for particular care if you have high blood pressure, a heart condition, or severe arteriosclerosis, and deeper tubs add water pressure, so people with heart conditions should be cautious about deep, full-body immersion. Gentler alternatives include a "warm bath" around 41°C, a longer soak in lukewarm water (about 36–38°C), or half-body and lower-body bathing, which ease the load on the heart and lungs while still warming you and supporting circulation. Older bathers, young children, and anyone unsteady on their feet should not bathe alone. Avoid bathing when feverish or unwell; pregnant women should be especially cautious with hot water. If you feel light-headed or unwell, get out — lowering your head as you leave — ask for help if needed, and rest.

When you first begin frequent hot-spring bathing, you may notice a temporary reaction known as yuatari within a few days to a week — loss of appetite, trouble sleeping, or skin irritation. If this happens, cut back on the number of baths until it passes.
