## What is a ryokan
A ryokan is a traditional Japanese inn — often described as one of the oldest forms of accommodation in the world, with roots dating back centuries. Ryokan are found throughout Japan and are especially common in hot-spring resorts. Rooms are Japanese-style (washitsu) with tatami-mat floors and futon bedding laid out on the floor at night, rather than Western beds. More than a place to sleep, a ryokan is a chance to experience traditional Japanese living and hospitality (omotenashi); inns range from small family-run houses to large hotel-like establishments. A typical stay with meals included runs roughly 15,000–30,000 yen per person per night.

## Yukata and footwear
On arrival you are given a yukata — a light cotton kimono-style robe — to wear around the inn and after bathing; staff will help explain how to wear it. Wrap the left side over the right (right over left is used only for the deceased) and tie it with the sash. Removing your shoes indoors is expected, and the inn provides slippers, geta, or zori to wear in hallways and gardens; slippers come off on tatami, and separate toilet slippers are used only in the bathroom.

## Meals: kaiseki and breakfast
Most ryokan stays include a multi-course kaiseki dinner — formal, seasonal Japanese cuisine made with local ingredients and beautifully presented — and a traditional Japanese breakfast the next morning. Meals are served either in your room or in a dining hall, often at a set time you choose at check-in. Some ryokan offer stays without meals, but dining in-house is considered an essential part of the experience. Tell the inn in advance about allergies or dietary needs, as menus are planned ahead.

## Bathing at a ryokan
Ryokan baths come in three main forms: large public communal baths (separated by gender), private or rental baths (kashikiri) you can reserve for your group, and private open-air or in-room baths fed by the spring. The usual onsen manners apply — bathe nude (no swimwear), wash thoroughly before entering, and note that tattoos may be restricted. Larger inns sometimes swap the men's and women's baths between evening and morning so guests can try both. You can bathe several times during your stay; before dinner, before bed, and after waking are all popular.

## Check-in, check-out, and the rhythm of a stay
Check-in is usually mid-afternoon and check-out fairly early, because staff prepare rooms and meals around set times. Futons are typically laid out by staff while you are at dinner and put away in the morning. A calm, unhurried pace is part of the experience — staff are welcoming and happy to explain the customs, so relax, enjoy the bath and meals, and follow the gentle rhythm the inn sets.
