## What is a ryokan
A ryokan is a traditional Japanese inn — often described as one of the oldest forms of accommodation in the world, with roots dating back centuries. Ryokan are found throughout Japan and are especially common in hot-spring resorts. Rooms are Japanese-style (washitsu) with tatami-mat floors and futon bedding laid out on the floor at night, rather than Western beds. More than a place to sleep, a ryokan is a chance to experience traditional Japanese living and hospitality (omotenashi); inns range from small family-run houses to large hotel-like establishments. A typical stay with meals included runs roughly 15,000–30,000 yen per person per night.

## The Japanese-style room (washitsu)
A ryokan room is a washitsu — a traditional room floored with tatami (rush) mats, whose fresh rush scent is part of the experience. Rooms are sized by the number of mats (for example 6-jo, 8-jo, 10-jo), and a single washitsu often serves as living room, dining room, and bedroom in turn. Typical features include a tokonoma, a slightly raised alcove displaying a hanging scroll, calligraphy, or an ikebana flower arrangement; fusuma and shoji, sliding doors and screens of wood and paper (shoji let in soft light but are delicate, so handle them gently); low seating such as a zataku table with zabuton cushions or legless zaisu chairs; and an oshiire closet that stores the bedding by day. At night, staff lay out the futon — a shikibuton mattress and kakebuton duvet — usually while you are at dinner. Always remove footwear before stepping onto the tatami. Many modern ryokan also offer "wa-modern" or Japanese-Western (wa-yo) rooms that pair tatami with Western beds and furniture.

## Yukata and footwear
On arrival you are given a yukata — a light cotton kimono-style robe — to wear around the inn and after bathing; staff will help explain how to wear it. Wrap the left side over the right (right over left is used only for the deceased) and tie it with the sash. Removing your shoes indoors is expected, and the inn provides slippers, geta, or zori to wear in hallways and gardens; slippers come off on tatami, and separate toilet slippers are used only in the bathroom.

## Meals: kaiseki and breakfast
Most ryokan stays include a multi-course kaiseki dinner — formal, seasonal Japanese cuisine made with local ingredients and beautifully presented — and a traditional Japanese breakfast the next morning. Meals are served either in your room or in a dining hall, often at a set time you choose at check-in. Some ryokan offer stays without meals, but dining in-house is considered an essential part of the experience. Tell the inn in advance about allergies or dietary needs, as menus are planned ahead.

## Bathing at a ryokan
Ryokan baths come in three main forms:

- **Private in-room baths** — a bath, sometimes an open-air rotenburo, inside your guest room, booked when you reserve. You can soak whenever and as long as you like without worrying about others, which suits families with young children and couples; the trade-off is less variety of baths.
- **Rental private baths (kashikiri-buro)** — baths outside the room reserved for your exclusive use by the hour, in varied designs (open-air, indoor, ocean-view). They may be free or cost around 2,000–3,000 yen, and are booked in one of three ways: first-come (use it when the door tag shows "vacant," 空室), reserved at check-in, or reserved in advance.
- **Large public communal baths** — gender-separated baths with indoor tubs, often an open-air rotenburo, and washing stations stocked with soap and shampoo. They offer the most authentic hot-spring atmosphere, but men and women cannot bathe together.

The usual onsen manners apply — bathe nude (no swimwear), shower thoroughly before entering, keep a small towel for modesty and look away from others, and note that tattoos may be restricted. Early morning and late evening tend to be the least crowded. Larger inns sometimes swap the men's and women's baths between evening and morning so guests can try both, and you can bathe several times during your stay — before dinner, before bed, and after waking are all popular.

## Check-in, check-out, and the rhythm of a stay
Check-in is usually mid-afternoon and check-out fairly early, because staff prepare rooms and meals around set times. Futons are typically laid out by staff while you are at dinner and put away in the morning. A calm, unhurried pace is part of the experience — staff are welcoming and happy to explain the customs, so relax, enjoy the bath and meals, and follow the gentle rhythm the inn sets.
