## What is a ryokan
A ryokan is a traditional Japanese inn, often built around an onsen. Rooms typically have tatami-mat floors, sliding paper doors, and a low table, with futon bedding laid out on the floor at night. Many onsen ryokan are destinations in themselves, combining the hot spring, seasonal cuisine, and quiet hospitality (omotenashi) into the whole stay.

## Yukata and slippers
On arrival you are usually given a yukata — a light cotton robe — to wear around the inn, including to and from the baths. Wrap the left side over the right (right over left is only used for the deceased), and tie it with the sash. Indoor slippers are worn in hallways but removed on tatami, and separate toilet slippers are used only in the bathroom. Wearing the yukata to dinner and the bath is normal and expected.

## Meals: kaiseki and breakfast
Many ryokan include a multi-course kaiseki dinner and a Japanese breakfast in the room rate. Kaiseki is a sequence of small, seasonal dishes served either in your room or a dining hall, often at a set time you choose at check-in. Tell the inn in advance about allergies or dietary needs, as menus are planned ahead and can be hard to change on the day.

## Bathing at a ryokan
Ryokan baths may be shared public baths (often separated by gender), and some offer private or family baths (kashikiri) you can reserve. Larger inns sometimes swap the men's and women's baths between evening and morning so guests can try both. Some rooms even include a private open-air bath fed by the spring. You can bathe multiple times during your stay — before dinner, before bed, and after waking are all popular.

## Check-in, check-out, and etiquette
Check-in is often mid-afternoon and check-out fairly early, because staff prepare rooms and meals around set times. Futons are usually laid out by staff while you are at dinner and put away in the morning. A calm, unhurried pace is part of the experience; relax, enjoy the bath and meals, and follow the gentle rhythm the inn sets.
