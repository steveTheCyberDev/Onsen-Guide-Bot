## General tattoo guidance
Tattoos have historically been associated with organised crime in Japan, so many traditional onsen and public baths display signs asking guests with tattoos not to enter. This is a general cultural norm, not a law, and attitudes are gradually relaxing as more international visitors arrive. Restrictions are still common but far from universal: a 2015 Japan Tourism Agency survey found about 30 percent of hotels and inns said they would allow guests with tattoos to use their communal baths. Policy varies widely from one facility to the next, so it is always worth checking a specific onsen's own rules before your visit rather than assuming. If you do not see a sign, simply ask whether tattoos are allowed.

**Source:** Japan National Tourism Organization (JNTO) — https://www.japan.travel/en/blog/japanese-Onsen-and-ink-the-tattoo-taboo/

## Options if you have tattoos
There are several common ways travellers with tattoos still enjoy an onsen. Smaller tattoos can often be covered with a waterproof skin-coloured patch, bandage, or cover sticker; some facilities even sell dedicated cover seals on-site and allow entry once tattoos are concealed, though the number and size permitted vary by facility, and a few now accept a rash guard or similar covering. Look for explicitly "tattoo-friendly" onsen, which openly welcome tattooed guests — these are still relatively rare but growing in number, and online portals and lists of tattoo-friendly facilities can help you find them. Public bathhouses (sento) often have fewer restrictions than large spa or "super sento" complexes. Choosing a facility outside the busiest tourist areas, or one attached to a hotel that caters to foreign visitors, can also help.

**Source:** Japan National Tourism Organization (JNTO) — https://www.japan.travel/en/blog/japanese-Onsen-and-ink-the-tattoo-taboo/ ; Nifty Onsen — https://onsen.nifty.com/ft-irezumi/

## Private and family baths (kashikiri)
The most reliable option is a private or rental family bath, known as kashikiri-buro or kazoku-buro. These are reserved for you (and your group) alone for a set time, so general tattoo rules do not apply. Many ryokan and onsen offer kashikiri baths for an extra fee or by reservation, and some guest rooms include a private in-room bath fed by the spring. This lets anyone bathe comfortably regardless of tattoos.

**Source:** Nifty Onsen — https://onsen.nifty.com/ft-irezumi/

## Important caveat
This guide gives general guidance only. It cannot tell you whether one particular onsen allows tattoos — that policy is set by each facility and changes over time. Always confirm directly with the specific onsen or your accommodation before relying on entry.
