## General tattoo guidance
Tattoos have historically been associated with organised crime in Japan, so many traditional onsen and public baths display signs asking guests with tattoos not to enter. This is a general cultural norm, not a law, and attitudes are gradually relaxing as more international visitors arrive. Policy varies widely from one facility to the next, so it is always worth checking a specific onsen's own rules before your visit rather than assuming.

## Options if you have tattoos
There are several common ways travellers with tattoos still enjoy an onsen. Small tattoos can often be covered with a waterproof skin-coloured patch or cover sticker, which some onsen accept. Look for explicitly "tattoo-friendly" onsen, which openly welcome tattooed guests. Choosing a facility outside the busiest tourist areas, or one attached to a hotel that caters to foreign visitors, can also help.

## Private and family baths (kashikiri)
The most reliable option is a private or family bath, known as kashikiri-buro or kazoku-buro. These are reserved for you (and your group) alone for a set time, so general tattoo rules do not apply. Many ryokan and onsen offer kashikiri baths for an extra fee or by reservation, and some guest rooms include a private in-room bath fed by the spring. This lets anyone bathe comfortably regardless of tattoos.

## Important caveat
This guide gives general guidance only. It cannot tell you whether one particular onsen allows tattoos — that policy is set by each facility and changes over time. Always confirm directly with the specific onsen or your accommodation before relying on entry.
