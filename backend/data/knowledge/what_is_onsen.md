## What counts as an onsen
Under Japanese law, water is defined as an onsen (hot spring) if it meets either of two conditions: it emerges from the ground at 25°C or higher, or it contains specified mineral components in regulated amounts. This means a spring can legally be an "onsen" even if it is not hot, as long as it has the required mineral content. The definition is about temperature or chemistry at the source, not about how warm the bath ultimately feels.

**Source:** Japan Secret Hot Springs Association — https://www.hitou.or.jp/static/spa_guide_main

## Volcanic and non-volcanic springs
Japan has an abundance of hot springs because of its volcanic geography. Onsen fall into two broad groups by origin: volcanic springs, heated by volcanic activity underground, and non-volcanic springs, which draw on sources such as deep groundwater or ancient "fossil" seawater trapped underground. Both can qualify as onsen under the legal definition above.

**Source:** Japan Secret Hot Springs Association — https://www.hitou.or.jp/static/spa_guide_main

## Kakenagashi vs. recirculated water
How an onsen manages its water is something many enthusiasts care about, and facilities generally fall along a spectrum:

- **Free-flowing from the source (gensen kakenagashi)** — fresh spring water flows continuously through the bath without being recirculated. This is the most prized style, valued for delivering the spring in its natural state.
- **Adjusted, non-recirculating** — fresh source water that is heated or diluted to a comfortable temperature, but still not recirculated.
- **Source water with recirculation** — fresh spring water is added while some water is recirculated to hold the temperature.
- **Filtered and recirculated** — fresh source water combined with filtration systems.
- **Fully recirculated** — the water is continuously recirculated, filtered, and sanitised.

Recirculation and sanitisation are common and practical, especially at larger facilities; "kakenagashi" simply signals that you are bathing in continuously fresh source water. Neither is "wrong" — it is a matter of style and preference.

**Source:** Japan Secret Hot Springs Association — https://www.hitou.or.jp/static/spa_guide_main

## Yutoji — the tradition of healing baths
Historically, people travelled to hot springs for "yutoji" (湯治): extended stays that combined the natural healing of the waters with rest and recovery, often in quiet, secluded places. This tradition is the cultural root of Japan's hot-spring culture, which the country's onsen associations work to preserve as a natural and cultural resource.
