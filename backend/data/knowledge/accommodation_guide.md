## Choosing where to stay on an onsen trip
There is no single "right" place to stay for an onsen trip — the main options trade off
atmosphere, price, meals, and convenience, and any of them can give you a good soak. The
common choices are the traditional inn (ryokan), the onsen hotel or resort hotel, the
business hotel with a large communal bath, and simpler family-run lodging (minshuku).

First, a key point that confuses many travellers: an **onsen and a ryokan are not the same
thing**. An onsen (温泉) is the hot-spring bath itself; a ryokan is a style of inn. The two
overlap but are independent — a ryokan with a hot spring is an "onsen ryokan," but many
ryokan have no onsen, and onsen also exist on their own in public bathhouses, hotels, and
day spas. So you do not have to stay in a ryokan to enjoy an onsen. Second, "having an
onsen" can mean two different things — a natural hot spring or a man-made bath — explained
in its own section below.

## Ryokan and onsen ryokan
A ryokan is a traditional Japanese inn — tatami-floored rooms, futon bedding, a yukata
robe, and usually a multi-course kaiseki dinner plus Japanese breakfast included in the
rate. Ryokan are especially common in hot-spring resort areas, where one located on a
natural hot spring is often called an "onsen ryokan." Staying at one is as much about the
experience — the meals, the baths, the unhurried pace — as about sleeping, and it is the
most immersive way to enjoy an onsen. A typical mid-range stay with meals runs roughly
15,000–30,000 yen per person per night, while higher-end and luxury ryokan can cost
considerably more. (See the ryokan guide for room, meal, and bathing detail.)

**Source:** JNTO — https://www.japan.travel/en/guide/japanese-ryokan/ ; Selected Onsen Ryokan — https://selected-ryokan.com/info/what-is-onsen-ryokan.html ; Japan Highlights — https://www.japanhighlights.com/japan/ryokan-and-hotel-differences

## Onsen hotels and resort hotels
In hot-spring areas, many lodgings are Western-style in name and layout — beds rather than
futon, a hotel front desk — yet still have hot-spring baths, and these are often called
"onsen hotels." So a place called "hotel" in an onsen town frequently does have an onsen;
the word "hotel" mainly signals Western-style rooms and a larger, more hotel-like operation,
not the absence of a bath. Because onsen exist independently of ryokan, a hotel or day spa
can offer a genuine hot-spring bath too. These suit travellers who want the hot-spring baths
and resort setting but prefer a bed and a less formal, less ritualised stay than a ryokan.

**Source:** Selected Onsen Ryokan — https://selected-ryokan.com/info/what-is-onsen-ryokan.html ; Japanese-Onsen.com — https://japanese-onsen.com/articles/onsen-vs-ryokan-what-is-the-difference/

## Business hotels with a large public bath (daiyokujo)
Business hotels are economical, no-frills lodgings, usually right by a train station, with
smaller rooms and fewer amenities than a resort hotel. Most have nothing more than an
in-room unit bath — but some chains build their whole appeal around a shared bathhouse. The
best-known example is the Dormy Inn chain, where properties feature a large communal bath
(daiyokujo) that typically includes an indoor bath, an open-air bath, and a sauna, often on
the top floor. Many Dormy Inn properties are explicitly branded as natural hot springs
("Natural Hot Spring … Dormy Inn", and the related "Onyado Nono" line), though not every
branch draws on a natural spring. This makes a business hotel a realistic way to enjoy a
communal hot-spring-style bath cheaply and flexibly, especially in cities, even when you are
not staying in an onsen resort.

**Source:** Dormy Inn — https://en.dormy-hotels.com/spa/ ; JNTO — https://www.japan.travel/en/ca/plan/accommodation/

## Natural onsen vs. artificial onsen (tennen vs. jinko)
"Onsen" does not always mean water straight from a hot spring. Under Japan's Onsen Law, a
natural hot spring (tennen onsen) is water that emerges from the ground at 25°C or above, or
that contains at least one of 19 designated mineral components. Where a natural source is
not available, a facility may instead heat ordinary water and add minerals to mimic a hot
spring — this is an artificial onsen (jinko onsen). Relatedly, a ryokan or other lodging
without geothermal access may offer a sento — a bath using ordinary heated tap water rather
than natural spring water. All of these are legitimate, common, and give you the
communal-bath experience; the difference is the source of the water, not the etiquette or
the enjoyment. If bathing in genuine spring water matters to you, look for "natural hot
spring" (天然温泉 / tennen onsen) in the property's description; if you simply want a relaxing
large bath, an artificial onsen or sento at a city hotel still does the job.

## Minshuku and simpler lodging
A minshuku is a family-owned guesthouse, common in resort and rural areas — similar in
spirit to a ryokan but simpler and less formal. Rates are usually per person and include
dinner and breakfast, rooms are plainly furnished, and guests often lay out their own
futon. Minshuku are a good-value, homely option, and those in hot-spring areas may have a
shared onsen bath, though facilities are more modest than at a ryokan or resort hotel.

**Source:** Oku Japan — https://www.okujapan.com/help/accommodation/ ; JNTO — https://www.japan.travel/en/ca/plan/accommodation/

## Ryokan vs. hotel: the practical differences
For an onsen trip the most common decision is ryokan vs. hotel. Typical contrasts:

- **Rooms & bedding** — ryokan rooms are usually larger (often around 30–40 m²) with tatami
  floors and futon laid on the floor; hotel rooms are more compact with raised Western beds.
- **Meals** — ryokan rates usually include both a kaiseki dinner and Japanese breakfast,
  often served in your room; hotels generally charge meals separately, typically a buffet
  breakfast with dinner not included.
- **Baths** — ryokan (especially in resort areas) often have both indoor and open-air baths
  with scenic views; city hotels more often have indoor-only baths, or none.
- **Price** — ryokan generally cost more per person than a comparable city or business
  hotel, in part because meals are included; luxury ryokan can run higher still.
- **Location & pace** — ryokan are often outside city centres in onsen/historic areas and
  set an unhurried, ritualised pace; hotels are usually central, flexible, and less formal.

A common approach is to spend 1–2 nights at a ryokan for the experience and use hotels for
the rest of a trip.

## Which should I choose?
As a rough guide:

- **For the full traditional experience** — kaiseki meals, tatami rooms, multiple baths, an
  unhurried pace — choose a **ryokan / onsen ryokan**. It is the most immersive and the most
  expensive, and best when the stay itself is the destination.
- **For hot-spring baths with a Western-style, more flexible stay** — beds, a hotel front
  desk, fewer rituals — choose an **onsen hotel / resort hotel** in a hot-spring area.
- **For a cheap, convenient soak, especially in a city or as a stopover** — choose a
  **business hotel with a large public bath** such as a Dormy Inn; you get a communal bath
  and sauna without resort prices, though it is not the immersive ryokan experience.
- **For good value and a homely, local feel** — choose a **minshuku**, accepting simpler
  rooms and facilities.

There is no wrong choice — it depends on whether you are prioritising the experience
(ryokan), comfort and flexibility (onsen/resort hotel), or budget and convenience (business
hotel, minshuku). For specific prices, availability, and each property's own bath and tattoo
policies, always check the individual facility, as these vary widely.
